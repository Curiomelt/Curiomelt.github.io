Why the US May Encounter Food Insecurity in the Future

Apr 8th 2023 7 Min read Author: Ethan Davis

Food insecurity has persistently plagued the United States, impacting countless individuals and families despite the country's affluence. Amidst a backdrop of poverty, income inequality, and limited access to affordable and nutritious food, there are several compelling reasons why the United States could face a future characterized by food insecurity.

Climate Change

One significant factor contributing to potential food insecurity in the United States is climate change. The impacts of this global phenomenon are already evident across the nation, manifesting as more frequent and severe weather events like droughts, floods, and wildfires. These calamities wreak havoc on crops, reducing yields and driving up food prices. Furthermore, rising temperatures pose challenges to traditional crop cultivation regions, resulting in diminished food production and availability.

For instance, the droughts and wildfires experienced in 2021 in California and Oregon—areas revered as the nation's breadbasket—severely affected the production of various crops, including almonds, grapes, and wine. These dire events, marked by droughts, heatwaves, and wildfires, also contributed to a decline in the population of essential pollinators, such as bees. Alarming predictions by the Union of Concerned Scientists suggest that by 2050, climate change could lead to a distressing 25 percent decline in U.S. agricultural productivity.

Pandemic

The ongoing global pandemic is yet another critical factor exacerbating the potential for food insecurity. COVID-19 has significantly impacted the food industry, causing disruptions in supply chains, surges in demand for specific products, and labor shortages. Consequently, prices for certain foods have escalated while others have become scarce. Moreover, the pandemic-induced economic downturn has resulted in widespread job losses, intensifying food insecurity among already vulnerable populations.

The pandemic has underscored the vital role of frontline workers, including farm laborers, in ensuring a functioning food system. Many of these individuals are undocumented immigrants, and the pandemic has magnified the challenges they face within their already precarious work conditions. As the number of undocumented workers in the agriculture industry decreases, the food supply chain could face further disruptions, amplifying future food insecurity concerns.

Income inequality

Income inequality stands as a formidable contributor to food insecurity in America. Despite economic growth in recent decades, wages for low- and middle-income workers have stagnated while the cost of living continues to soar. This confluence of factors has made it increasingly arduous for numerous families to afford nutritious food, particularly in areas where access to grocery stores and fresh produce is limited. Consequently, many individuals resort to inexpensive, processed foods that are calorie-dense but lacking in nutritional value.

A 2021 report by Feeding America revealed that even before the pandemic, 35 million Americans wrestled with hunger. As the pandemic persists, the number of struggling families is expected to rise. Furthermore, food insecurity disproportionately affects communities of color and rural areas, where the dearth of access to healthy food exacerbates the problem.

Political Climate

The political climate in the United States also poses potential challenges to future food security. The current administration has proposed cuts to vital food assistance programs such as SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program), which could leave millions of Americans without access to essential nutrition. Additionally, the ongoing debates surrounding immigration policy and the treatment of undocumented workers could exacerbate labor shortages within the agriculture industry, further impeding the stability of the food supply chain.

In conclusion, the United States faces a convergence of factors that could propel the nation towards increased food insecurity in the future. Climate change, the COVID-19 pandemic, income inequality, and political uncertainty all pose significant threats to the food supply chain and the accessibility of affordable, nutritious food for millions of Americans. To avert this impending crisis, policymakers must take proactive measures to address these challenges. This includes investing in sustainable agricultural practices, expanding access to healthy food in underserved areas, and safeguarding the integrity of food assistance programs.
